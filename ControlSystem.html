The simulation:

Let the arm be defined by the state vector \(\mathbf{q}=\begin{bmatrix}\theta_1\\ \theta_2\end{bmatrix}\), where \(\theta_1\) is defined relative to the horizontal axis and \(\theta_2\) is the angle between the segments. Furthermore, let segment \(i\) of the arm have a length \(l_i\), a mass \(m_i\), and a moment of inertia \(I_i\). Then the position vector of the end of the arm is \(\mathbf{x}=\begin{bmatrix}l_1\cos(\theta_1)+l_2\cos(\theta_1+\theta_2)\\ l_1\sin(\theta_1)+l_2\sin(\theta_1+\theta_2)\end{bmatrix}\).
Let there be two inputs present, \(\tau_1\) and \(\tau_2\), applied to the first and second joint of the structure, respectively.
In order to derive the equations of motion for this system, we need to express the kinetic and potential energy in terms of the state vector \(\mathbf{q}\) and its derivative, \(\mathbf{\dot{q}}\).
The potential energy of the system is \(\displaystyle V=\sum_{i=1}^2 m_igy_i\), while the kinetic energy is \(T=\sum_{i=1}^2 (\frac{1}{2}m_iv_i^2+\frac{1}{2}I_i\omega_i^2)\). In order to express these in terms of our state vector, we must define:

The controller:

Let a controller be designed so that a user may select a point within the reach of the arm \((x,y)\) relative to the arm's base for the tip of the arm to reach.
One example of such a controller is the Jacobian Transpose Controller. This utilizes the Jacobian of the system to convert back and forth between task space (Cartesian coordinates) and system space (\(\mathbf{q}\)). Given that \(\mathbf{x}=f(\mathbf{q})\), we can find \(\dot{\mathbf{x}}=J\dot{\mathbf{q}}\), where J is the Jacobian of f, by the chain rule.
$$J=\begin{bmatrix}-l_1\sin(\theta_1)-l_2\sin(\theta_1+\theta_2) & -l_2\sin(\theta_1+\theta_2) \\ l_1\cos(\theta_1)+l_2\cos(\theta_1+\theta_2) & l_2\cos(\theta_1+\theta_2)\end{bmatrix}$$ By defining a virtual force \(F=K_pe-K_d\dot{x}\) generated with a PD controller in task space, we can convert to an applied torque with \(\tau=J^TF\). However, this produces a steady-state error due to gravity, which can be fixed by modifying the torque equation with gravity compensation. The final torque is then \(\tau=J^TF+G(q)\).
